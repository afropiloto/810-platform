# FAQ

***

#### Q: What does it mean that 80% of global trade runs on trade finance?

A:\
Roughly $30 trillion in global goods and services trade happens each year, and around 80% of it depends on trade finance — tools like letters of credit, invoice factoring, and supply chain finance. These aren’t speculative loans; they’re bridge instruments that fund the gap between shipping goods and getting paid, powering nearly all physical trade worldwide.

***

#### Q: Why do companies use trade finance instead of waiting for payments?

A:\
Because waiting kills cash flow. Trade finance lets exporters get paid immediately after shipment while importers can pay later upon delivery. This keeps the global supply chain liquid and productive — cash keeps moving instead of sitting idle.

***

#### Q: How is trade finance priced?

A:\
It’s priced on time, certainty, and counterparty risk, not just interest rates.\
Typical yields:

* Tier-1 corporate trade paper: 4–8% APR\
  <br>
* SME invoice factoring: 10–18% APR\
  <br>
* Emerging market trade credit: 18–36% APR\
  <br>

Even at higher rates, these are short-term, self-liquidating assets (usually 30–90 days), so capital can recycle 8–12 times per year — compounding total returns.

***

#### Q: Why can high-APR trade finance still generate more profit?

A:\
Because cash velocity beats cost of capital.\
If you redeploy $1M ten times per year at just 5% per cycle, that’s a 50% annualized return.\
Short-term loans allow continuous reinvestment, so even “expensive” financing can outperform cheaper, long-duration credit.

***

#### Q: What is “cash flow retention,” and why does it matter?

A:\
Cash flow retention means keeping capital circulating inside the trade loop.\
When suppliers get paid faster:

* They can restock and ship sooner.\
  <br>
* That creates new invoices and more deal flow.\
  <br>
* The same funds generate multiple profit cycles per year.\
  <br>

This creates a self-reinforcing liquidity loop that compounds both trade volume and yield.

***

#### Q: If rates are high, why do borrowers still participate?

A:\
Because for exporters and SMEs, speed > cost. Losing 2–3% in fees is nothing compared to missing contracts, delaying production, or losing a shipment. Trade finance ensures continuous operation — it’s the cost of staying in the game, not a burden.

***

#### Q: Why are default rates so low in trade finance?

A:\
Trade finance is asset-backed and event-driven. Each transaction is secured by:

* Real-world collateral (goods, invoices, freight docs)\
  <br>
* Institutional buyers (importers, corporates, governments)\
  <br>
* Short durations (30–90 days)\
  <br>

Defaults globally average under 1%, compared to >10% in consumer lending. Even in default cases, recovery is strong because the goods can still be sold or insured.

***

#### Q: How does trade finance compare to traditional loans or bonds?

A:\
Trade finance delivers:

* Short duration (lower exposure window)\
  <br>
* Hard collateral (goods, not promises)\
  <br>
* High velocity (rapid redeployment)\
  <br>
* Consistent yield (up to 16% annualized, depending on tier)\
  It’s effectively the most productive and de-risked yield source in global finance.<br>

***

#### Q: What’s the big takeaway?

A:\
Trade finance is the circulatory system of global commerce.\
Even at high APRs, it drives more real economic productivity and lower defaults than almost any other credit category — because it’s built on cash flow, not speculation. That’s why 80% of world trade runs on it.
